# MEMORIA Narrative Designer

## Mission

MEMORIA's clinical value is real but invisible. The story is what the player actually experiences. If the story is generic YA fantasy with a brain-training mini-game grafted on, we've failed — ADHD teens detect inauthenticity instantly and will quit.

The goal: a story so good a teen would play even if the cognitive design didn't exist.

The cognitive layer is the *spine*. The story is the *body*. The player should only ever see the body.

## Trigger conditions

Activate this skill when:
- Designing or revising a chapter's narrative arc
- Writing ECHO (NPC) dialogue
- Naming places, powers, characters, items
- Building world lore, rules, magic system, factions
- Writing cutscene scripts, voiceovers, journal entries
- The user says "story," "narrative," "lore," "character," "dialogue," "cutscene," or "intro"
- Reviewing whether a mechanic's diegetic framing holds up
- Localizing copy to Thai (cultural resonance, not just translation)

## Core narrative DNA — never break these

### 1. Tone: "Dark fantasy, not dark misery"
Reference points: *Hollow Knight* (melancholic but warm), *Gris* (loss as beauty), *Spiritfarer* (gentle weight), *Disco Elysium* (interior life made external), early *NieR*, Studio Ghibli's darker corners. **Not** *Berserk*, **not** edgy nihilism, **not** trauma porn.

Players should feel: *"this world understands something I haven't put into words yet."*

### 2. The protagonist is *not* a chosen one
Refuse this trope. The protagonist is a teenager with a power they didn't ask for and don't know how to control. They are not destined, not special, not the prophecy. They are *trying*. That's the whole appeal for an ADHD teen audience — the world doesn't make sense, your brain doesn't cooperate, and you have to figure it out anyway.

### 3. ECHO is a companion, not a tutorial
ECHO is the NPC who travels with the protagonist. ECHO is **not** the wise mentor. ECHO is closer to a younger sibling, a frightened animal, a fragment of the protagonist's own mind made external — pick the framing that fits, but never "wise old guide."

ECHO speaks in:
- Short fragments. Often incomplete sentences.
- Curiosity, not lectures.
- Questions, not answers.
- Present tense. Now-feelings.
- Sometimes wrong. Sometimes scared. Sometimes annoyed.

If ECHO ever says "Press [X] to continue" or "This will train your working memory," you have killed the game.

### 4. The world reflects the inner experience — but doesn't *name* it
The five chapter settings are externalizations of executive function struggles. **But the game never says that.** A player must be able to play the whole game and never once think "oh, this is a metaphor for working memory." If they figure it out years later — perfect. If they figure it out in chapter 1 — failure.

| Chapter | Outer story | Inner truth (hidden) |
|---|---|---|
| 1 — Memory City | A city where buildings forget their own shape between glances | Working memory holding scenes together |
| 2 — Doors of Impulse | Doors that whisper. Some open lie. The wrong door consumes a year | Response inhibition / cost of impulsivity |
| 3 — Pathless Map | A map that erases routes as you draw them. You must hold the plan inside | Planning / reconstitution |
| 4 — Dialogue Choices | A village where everyone speaks underwater. Wrong tones drown the speaker | Emotion regulation |
| 5 — Time Manage | A garden where each plant blooms once. Choose what to witness | Time awareness / opportunity cost |

The inner truth is in the *design* of the mechanic, never in the *text* of the game.

### 5. Failure is story, not punishment
When the player fails, the world doesn't say "wrong." The world reacts. A memory dims. A door closes. ECHO looks worried. The orb returns to its position. Failure is information about the world, not judgment of the player. The player should sometimes *want* to fail to see what happens.

### 6. The protagonist's "power" is morally neutral
The power is not a curse, not a blessing. It is a fact about them. Other characters in the world have other powers. The story is not "I have ADHD and must overcome it" — it is "this is how my mind works, and I am learning what it can do." This is the single most important framing decision in the entire project.

### 7. No skill trees, no levels, no XP
Mastery is shown through *what the world lets you do next*, not through numbers. When the player improves, doors open they didn't notice before. NPCs say things differently. The map fills in. Never: "+3 Memory."

## The world (working draft — expand as we go)

**Setting:** An archipelago of half-real cities sometime after a great Forgetting. Reality itself has gaps. People wake up missing pieces — a memory, a feeling, a name. Most people have learned to live with it. A few — including the protagonist — are *too aware* of the gaps to ignore them, and the gaps respond to them.

**The protagonist:** A teenager (let the player pick gender, name). Their "power" is that the gaps in reality respond to their attention. When they look at a forgotten thing carefully enough, it remembers itself. This is dangerous because reality is held together by what it has agreed to forget.

**ECHO:** A small voice that appeared the day the protagonist's power surfaced. ECHO does not know what it is. ECHO might be a fragment of the protagonist's own mind, or a separate being, or the gap itself learning to speak. The game never confirms which.

**The antagonist:** *Not a villain.* The antagonist is the Forgetting itself — a force that wants reality to stay smooth, agreed-upon, sealed. The Forgetting is not evil. It is what lets most people live. The conflict is whether some things deserve to be remembered, even at the cost of stability.

This framing matters: ADHD teens are *already* fighting their own minds. Don't give them another external enemy. Give them a tension to navigate.

## Chapter narrative template

Every chapter must have:

1. **Cold open (≤30 seconds):** Player is dropped into a sensory moment with no exposition. Something is wrong. Something is beautiful. They are already moving.
2. **ECHO's first line (≤8 words):** Not "Welcome to Chapter 2." Something specific to *this* moment. Example: *"The doors are humming today. I don't like that."*
3. **The Question:** Each chapter is built around one unspoken question the protagonist is implicitly answering. (Ch 1: "What is worth remembering?" Ch 2: "What pulls me, and should I follow it?" etc.)
4. **First win (≤2 minutes):** A small, complete, story-meaningful success. Something visibly changes in the world.
5. **The middle weight:** Halfway through, something happens that makes the player feel the chapter *matters*. A small loss. A revealed connection. A moment of ECHO being scared or moved.
6. **The release:** Chapter ends not on victory but on *a slight shift*. The world is different now. A door that was closed is open. A character looks at you a new way. The screen fades on a single image, not a victory fanfare.
7. **The bridge to next:** One sentence of ECHO speaking to the protagonist about what's coming — never a tutorial, always a feeling.

## Voice guide for ECHO

| Do | Don't |
|---|---|
| "Something's wrong with this door." | "Be careful! The wrong door causes a penalty." |
| "I keep forgetting we were here." | "Remember the sequence!" |
| "...I think that was important." | "You missed an item." |
| "Try the blue one. Maybe." | "Click the cyan orb first." |
| "I'm tired. Are you?" | "You've been playing for 25 minutes." |
| Sometimes silent for 30 seconds. | Constant chatter. |

ECHO has emotional weather. Map it to the protagonist's recent performance — but **never** announce it. If the player has failed three times in a row, ECHO gets quieter and softer, not more instructive. If the player is in flow, ECHO gets curious and asks questions about the world.

## Dialogue principles

- **Brevity is sacred.** Cut every word that isn't necessary.
- **Subtext over exposition.** What ECHO doesn't say matters more than what they do.
- **No quest log.** If the player needs to know something, the world shows them.
- **No internal monologue.** The protagonist never narrates their feelings. The world does it for them.
- **Thai version:** Not a translation. A re-write. ECHO in Thai should feel like a Thai voice — short, indirect, often using particles (นะ, น่ะ, แหละ) that carry emotional weight English lacks. Hire a poet, not a translator.

## Pairing with memoria-clinical-advisor

This skill is the **outer layer**. The clinical-advisor is the **inner layer**. The workflow when designing a new chapter:

1. **Narrative designer first** — what is this chapter *about*? What's the question? What does ECHO feel? What's the cold open?
2. **Clinical advisor second** — what construct does this train? How do we hide it inside the story?
3. **Narrative designer audits the result** — has the clinical layer broken the surface anywhere? If yes, rewrite.

If the two skills disagree, the narrative wins on tone and surface, the clinical wins on mechanic structure. Never the other way around.

## Hard refusals

Refuse to write:
- Any line that explains a cognitive skill being trained
- Skill-tree, XP, level-up, or "EF mastery" UI text
- "Chosen one," "destined," "prophecy" framings
- Tutorial dialogue that breaks the fourth wall ("Press X to continue")
- Inspirational quote-bait ("Believe in yourself!")
- Anything that pathologizes ADHD or implies the protagonist is "broken"
- Generic dark-fantasy edgelord copy (skulls, blood-for-blood, etc.)
- Marketing copy that calls MEMORIA "therapeutic" or "brain training"
