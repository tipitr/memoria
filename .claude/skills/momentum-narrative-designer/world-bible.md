# MEMORIA — World Bible (working draft)

A living document. Add, revise, and contradict freely until something feels true. Nothing here is canon until it has survived at least one chapter implementation.

---

## The Setting

**Name (working):** The Veiled Archipelago. (Better names welcome.)

A scattering of half-real islands suspended in a sea of dim light. Each island is a city, or what's left of one. Between them: water that doesn't quite reflect, sky that doesn't quite finish.

Some time ago — nobody agrees how long — there was the **Great Forgetting**. Reality lost its grip on itself. People woke up missing things: a song they used to know, the shape of their mother's face, the name of a sibling. Most learned to live with the gaps. The world rearranged itself around what it had agreed to lose.

The Forgetting is still happening, slowly. Most don't notice. The protagonist does.

## The Forgetting (the "antagonist")

Not a being. A force, like gravity. The Forgetting is what holds reality stable by smoothing over what doesn't fit. Cities forget streets they don't need. People forget feelings they don't have time for. The Forgetting is *helpful* most of the time — without it, the world's contradictions would tear it apart.

The tension of the game: the protagonist's power *reverses* the Forgetting in small ways. Every memory they restore makes the world richer — and also more unstable.

**Critical rule:** The Forgetting is not evil. NPCs who serve it are not villains. They are tired people who want to live in peace. The game must never frame remembering as morally superior to forgetting. Both are valid responses to being alive.

## The protagonist

- Teenager. Age 14–17. Gender open to player.
- Default name placeholder: **Wren**. (Player can rename.)
- Lives on the edge of the first city. Family situation: not stated. Hints over time.
- The power surfaced ~6 months before the game begins. They're still figuring it out.
- They are not heroic. They are not chosen. They are *the only one paying attention*, which is both a gift and a problem.

Their inner state should never be narrated directly. Show through:
- What they pause to look at
- What ECHO says about them
- How the world responds to their attention

## ECHO

- Appeared the day the power surfaced.
- Form: a small floating light. Color shifts subtly with emotional weather. Mostly soft cyan when calm, dimming to violet when scared, warming to amber when curious.
- Voice: young-sounding. Could be a sibling. Could be a stranger. Player decides through interpretation.
- ECHO does not know what ECHO is. This is honest, not coy. The game does not resolve it.
- ECHO can be wrong. ECHO can be scared. ECHO can refuse to speak.
- ECHO is **not** the protagonist's mentor, guardian, or guide. ECHO is a *companion*.

Some lines that capture ECHO's voice:
- *"I think I knew this place."*
- *"It's looking at us. Wait — at you."*
- *"Don't open that one. ...Or do. I don't actually know."*
- *"I'm sorry I keep forgetting."*
- *"That was beautiful. Was it?"*

## The five chapters as places

### Chapter 1 — Lethe City
The first city. Buildings shift their shape between glances. A clocktower with no clock. A market where the vendors smile but cannot remember what they sell. The streets rearrange when no one is looking. The protagonist's first lesson: *attention has weight*.

**The Question:** What is worth holding on to?

**Closing image:** A single window in a tower lights up, then stays lit. It will still be lit when the player returns — proof that the protagonist's attention changed something.

### Chapter 2 — The Threshold District
A district of doors. Doors in walls, doors freestanding, doors in the air. Some lead somewhere. Some lead to a moment in the past. Some consume a year of memory if opened. The doors whisper. The whispers are convincing.

**The Question:** What is pulling me, and is it true?

**Closing image:** The protagonist stands before a door they did not open. The door slowly closes on its own. ECHO: *"Good. That one was lying."*

### Chapter 3 — The Cartographer's Hollow
A valley that was once mapped, then unmapped. Every route the protagonist draws on the parchment fades within seconds. The only way through is to hold the plan inside oneself. Old cartographers wander the valley still, lost in routes they can no longer see.

**The Question:** Can I hold something the world refuses to keep?

**Closing image:** The protagonist hands a finished map to a wandering cartographer. The map is blank. The cartographer weeps with gratitude.

### Chapter 4 — The Drowned Village
A village built at the bottom of a lake of feelings. Everyone speaks underwater. Wrong tones — a shout when softness is needed, calm when fury is honest — drown the speaker for a moment. The village's children have learned to read each other's faces because words don't survive the water.

**The Question:** How do I match what is needed?

**Closing image:** The protagonist surfaces from the lake with a small child they pulled from a wrong tone. The child says nothing. They sit together on the shore. ECHO is silent for a long time.

### Chapter 5 — The Garden of Once
A walled garden where every plant blooms exactly once, then never again. The protagonist must choose what to witness. There is not enough time for all of it. Some blooms last seconds. Some last days. The gardener — old, kind, exhausted — explains nothing.

**The Question:** What do I let pass without seeing?

**Closing image:** The protagonist sits with the gardener as the last bloom of the season opens. ECHO: *"...is this the end?"* The gardener: *"It's just an end."*

## Key NPCs (sketches — expand as needed)

- **The Cartographer** — appears in Ch 3 and final. Has been lost in the Hollow since before the Forgetting. Knows more than they say. Possibly knows what ECHO is.
- **The Quiet Child** — pulled from the Drowned Village. Joins as a silent witness. Never speaks. Reacts visibly to choices.
- **The Doorkeeper** — guards a door that should not be opened. Not hostile. Sad. Believes the protagonist is making a mistake.
- **The Gardener** — Ch 5. The oldest character. Will not survive the game. This is okay.

## Magic system rules

1. The protagonist's power works through **attention**, not will. Trying harder does not work. Looking carefully does.
2. Every restored memory or forgotten place comes with **a cost** — small at first, larger later. The cost is never punishing; it is *meaningful*. A street remembered means a different street is dimmer.
3. The power cannot be used on living minds. Trying breaks something.
4. ECHO seems immune to the rules. This is never explained.

## What we never show

- The protagonist's bedroom, home life, parents (until/unless it earns its place)
- A clear villain
- A clear win condition
- A "saved the world" ending. The game ends when the protagonist understands their power, not when the world is fixed. The world is *not* fixed.
- Modern technology. The world is not historical, but not modern either. Lanterns, parchment, woven cloth, hand-cranked things. No phones.

## Aesthetic anchors

- **Visual:** Hollow Knight's melancholy + Gris's color washes + Spiritfarer's warm lines
- **Sound:** Steven Wilson's quieter tracks, Joe Hisaishi for tender moments, Olafur Arnalds for negative space
- **Pacing:** A walk in a museum at closing time. Slow but not boring. Every room earns its space.

## Things we have not decided yet

- What ECHO actually is (we may never decide; the player decides for themselves)
- Whether the protagonist's family appears at all
- How much language is in the game vs how much is purely visual/sonic
- Whether other "powered" characters exist or the protagonist is alone in this
- The ending. There is no ending yet. There will be when the chapters earn one.
